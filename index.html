Crisis

The 2010 post-election crisis breaks out in Côte d’Ivoire when sitting President Laurent Gbagbo refuses to acknowledge defeat in the presidential elections to Alassane Ouattara former commander of the rebels in the north. After four months three thousand people are dead and hundreds of thousands of others are displaced. While the violence has subsided, Côte d’Ivoire continues to face serious challenges as it emerges from more than ten years of civil conflict.   


Fighting stops in the spring of 2011 and Ouattara takes office on May 6th, 2011.




OTI launches CITI program in September with the goal of supporting the new government’s capacity to meet the needs of all Ivoirians, and promoting social cohesion through citizen participation in the recovery process.

Phases


Phase I: First 6-12 months of the program focused on helping the Ivorian state reestablish its presence and services throughout the country. This included:
     • Equipment drops to media outlets
     • Open houses with regional and local courthouses to explain legal services; and
     • Town hall meetings informing the population of the government’s return

Phase II: Year 2 of the program evolved to help people talk to each other within their communities and rebuild community trust and dialogue. This included:
     • Supporting nascent civil society to return as well as support the emergence of more independent, less politicized civil society more cohesive of diverse communities

Phase III: Year 3 of the program worked to get people to talk to each other about sensitive issues. This also included engaging sensitive groups including ex-combatants, youth, and non-Ivorian communities.

Training, Facilities, and Equipment for Journalists
CITI supported communal radios Duekoué, Guiglo, Toulepleu, Bin Houye, Daloa, Youpougon, and Sakassou which were struggling to emerge from social instability because of their inability to access dependable information about current events rather than rumors and hearsay.
Support to Ivorian Press Agency (AIP) improved the capacity of Ivoirian journalists to provide real-time reliable information through training, equipment, and office rehabilitation.

Current Affairs Programs Addressing Local Concerns
Building on previous successes, CITI helped radio stations in Bouaké, Duékoué, Korhogo, and Yopougon produce radio programming that addresses the local populations’ concerns. - As a result, four stations – Radio Media Plus (Bouaké), Radio Satellite (Korhogo), Radio Fraternité (Yopougon), et Radio Voix du Guémon (Duékoué) – are each producing a local current affairs program. Though the training only took place in early March 2014, the change in programming has already shown significant results: - Two episodes produced by Radio Satellite on local issues in Korhogo have prompted the authorities to take action: 1) illegal trash dump in Korhogo residential neighborhood. 2) reasons behind the illegal use of sidewalks for commercial activity.

Making People Count through Improved Birth Registry

Due to the absence of state services following the division of the country in 2002, a significant number of people in northern Côte d'Ivoire were never registered at birth, never obtained any form of state identification, and are not included on state rolls. A lack of access to the government registration system continues to keep official registration numbers low, resulting in an ongoing lack of access to services, including health care and education, and feelings of exclusion. To address this problem, OTI supported registration centers in the Gbêkê region with equipment and training to increase community access to registration services and through a mobile registration campaign. Additional training was also provided to civil society to encourage registration, explain requirements, and to clarify individual and parental responsibilities. As a result of these efforts, other regions and prefects are looking to Gbêkê and Bouaké as an example of how to improve registration services and better serve their communities.

IMPROVED
GOVERNANCE

Restoring Citizen's Confidence in the Judicial System

In support of the Ministry of Justice's effort to revitalize the justice system, CITI support to a series of judicial "open houses" that helped put a human face to the justice system. A history of corruption and dysfunction, as well as misconceptions of the role of the judiciary, has discouraged many Ivorians from trusting the resolution of local conflicts to the formal justice system. Instead, grievances are more likely to be settled in parallel and less-impartial customary courts. Women, in particular, frequently complain of inequities in the treatment of complaints over rights of inheritance or access to land when brought before customary courts. The focus of the exchange was on how the judiciary works and the services available to communities through the court and its staff but the court staff also offered free legal consultations in partnership with local community advocacy groups. These open houses helped the community understand and access judicial institutions and were an important steps towards ensuring the delivery of transparent and equitable justice.

MITIGATE
COMMUNITY TENSIONS

USAID contributes to the financial empowerment of women in Abobo

In collaboration with the Abobo mayor's office, OTI helped organize an open house to help women who greatly suffered during the crisis, including many recently widowed, to address obstacles obtaining financial loans. More than 300 women traders gathered at the city hall to seek out information about and submit applications for loans. One loan recipient, Bintou Cissé, a widow and mother of five, is a prime example of the impact of this support. With a $1,000 USD loan, she started to expand her business of dyeing and selling traditional fabric. Already her income has doubled to $160 per month. She is reinvesting her extra income in her business and states she can now envision sending all of her children to school. "This open house has been a blessing for us; I had a completely different mindset and would have never even thought of going to a microfinance institution to ask for loans. Today, I am earning more money and I was able to hire one more woman to help... I will send the rest of my children to school next year if God wills" she said.

January 2014:

Is the transition consolidated?


Significant progress has been made towards economic recovery and political stability after the 2011 electoral crisis. Since assuming the presidency in May 2011, Alassane Ouattara has promoted economic growth, extended state authority and services across the country, and overseen relatively peaceful legislative and local elections in 2012 and 2013, respectively.



Nevertheless the underlying causes of the crisis have been only partially addressed, allowing community divisions to deepen and political tensions to simmer.


Why the 2015 Elections matter


Since 1990 elections have been major sources of instability and violence in Côte d’Ivoire. The Presidential elections in 2015 could emerge as an incendiary flashpoint, bringing to the fore unresolved issues such as nationality/citizenship, ethnicity, land tenure and impunity that were at the heart of the crisis and remain impediments to a successful post transition period. There is concern that these issues could be exploited particularly at the community level to disrupt and undermine the elections process and gains made during the recovery period as well as impede progress towards consolidating and achieving additional political and economic reforms.



Looking Forward: CITI-2


Given these issues, the second phase of USAID/OTI’s Côte d’Ivoire Transition Initiatives (CITI) program, CITI 2, will focus on increasing community cohesion and resilience and providing gap support to the electoral process with a view towards local-level issues. Taking advantage of key partnerships with local government institutions and non-governmental organizations, and utilizing OTI’s ability to rapidly design and implement activities through its small-grant mechanism, CITI 2 will concentrate on finding grassroots solutions to potential flashpoint issues while continuing to strengthen relationships across and within communities.
